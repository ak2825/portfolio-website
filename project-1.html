Beats and Beliefs

Does freedom of speech shape what music a country listens to? An analysis of Spotify Top 50 chart data merged with country-level democracy indices.

Python R SQL Data Cleaning EDA Regression Analysis GitHub

Overview

This project explored whether a country's level of political freedom influences what kind of music charts there. Specifically: do countries with lower freedom of speech indices tend to have fewer explicit songs in their Spotify Top 50?

The project also examined the dominance of U.S. and English-language content in global music consumption, investigating whether "globalization" in music is actually just Americanization.

The Data

I worked with two datasets: Spotify's Top 50 charts for multiple countries (including track metadata such as explicit flag, artist, and chart position) and country-level democracy/freedom indices from a public political science dataset.

The main data challenges were:

  • Inconsistent country name formatting between the two datasets required careful string matching and manual mapping in SQL
  • The Spotify data had missing explicit flags for some tracks, which required imputation decisions
  • Some countries had very few charting tracks, creating small-sample issues for regression

My Approach

I queried and cleaned the Spotify data using SQL to extract explicit content rates per country, then merged it with the democracy index data in Python using Pandas. I filtered to countries with at least 10 charting tracks to ensure statistical validity.

Exploratory analysis in R (ggplot2) revealed clear regional clustering — English-speaking countries had significantly higher explicit rates regardless of their democracy scores, suggesting language is a confounding variable.

I ran multivariate regression in Python/R controlling for English-language dominance, GDP per capita, and internet penetration rate to isolate the freedom-of-speech effect.

Results

After controlling for confounds, freedom of speech score had a statistically significant positive relationship with explicit content rates (p < 0.05). However, the effect size was modest — the biggest predictor of a country's music content was whether English-language artists dominated its charts.

The analysis found that roughly 60% of all charting tracks across non-English-speaking countries were by U.S. or UK artists, suggesting that what looks like "global music diversity" is largely English-language cultural export.
