Allington Lab Research

Generating high-quality labeled training data for a deep learning land-use classification model using satellite imagery of agricultural land in Uttarakhand, India.

QGIS Python Deep Learning Geospatial Data Labeling

Overview

As a Research Assistant at Cornell CALS's Allington Lab, I contribute to a project building an automated land-use classification system for detecting active vs. abandoned agricultural land in the Uttarakhand region of northern India. The model is designed to help researchers and policymakers track agricultural abandonment patterns at scale — a problem that can't be solved manually given the geographic scope.

My role focuses on the data labeling pipeline: producing the ground-truth training data that the deep learning model learns from.

The Data

The input data is multi-temporal satellite imagery — images of the same parcels taken at different points in the growing season. The key insight is that active agricultural land shows distinct phenological patterns (crop growth cycles) that can be distinguished from fallow or abandoned land when you compare images across time.

Data challenges I navigated:

  • Cloud cover obscured many images, requiring careful selection of usable time windows
  • Field boundaries in hilly terrain are irregular and difficult to delineate precisely
  • Visual distinction between fallow (temporarily unplanted) and abandoned (permanently unused) land requires domain judgment about phenology and field morphology

My Approach

I used QGIS to view and annotate satellite imagery, drawing polygon boundaries around individual agricultural parcels and labeling each as active cultivation, fallow, or abandoned. Each annotation required visual inference over crop phenology (green vegetation in growing season), spatial texture (tilled vs. overgrown surfaces), and field morphology (maintained vs. eroded boundaries).

To ensure temporal consistency — meaning the same parcel is labeled the same way across different image dates — I developed a personal review workflow: annotating candidate parcels in multiple time windows before finalizing a label, and flagging ambiguous cases for supervisor review rather than guessing.

Results

I have produced hundreds of quality-controlled polygon annotations that are feeding into the model's training pipeline. The annotations enable downstream geospatial preprocessing in QGIS and improve model generalization — the model's ability to correctly classify parcels it hasn't seen before.

This is ongoing research. The broader goal is a deployable classification tool that can scan entire districts for agricultural land-use change without requiring manual survey visits.
